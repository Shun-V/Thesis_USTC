\section{Systematics and Uncertainties}
\label{sec:monoZ-syst}

The evaluation of systematic uncertainties within this search comprehensively accounts for both theoretical modeling limitations and experimental detector effects.

Regarding theoretical modeling systematics, the employed strategies strictly adhere to the established prescriptions provided by the ATLAS Physics Modelling Group. The primary sources of these theoretical uncertainties are categorized as follows:
\begin{itemize}

\item To assess the impact of missing higher-order QCD corrections, the renormalisation ($\mu_R$) and factorisation ($\mu_F$) scales are varied. This is achieved via a correlated three-point scheme, where both scales are concurrently multiplied by factors of 0.5 or 2.0 to derive the downward and upward systematic shifts, respectively. The corresponding matrix-element event weights, inherently provided within the generated MC samples, are utilized to quantify these effects, yielding overall QCD scale uncertainties between 4\% and 5\% across various signal models.

\item Uncertainties originating from the parton shower and hadronisation modeling are estimated by applying respective eigenvector variations of the \textsc{Pythia}~8 A14 tune (applicable to processes such as top-quark pair production). Conversely, for datasets simulated utilizing the \textsc{Sherpa} framework (including diboson and specific signal processes), comparisons against alternative truth-level configurations are employed. The magnitude of these showering uncertainties generally spans from 1\% to 4\% depending on the specific process.

\item Specific higher-order electroweak (EW) corrections are applied to the $\text{ZZ}$ continuum and the signal samples, following the methodology outlined in preceding sections. The systematic uncertainty assigned to these EW correction factors is approximately 2\%.

\item To gauge the modeling systematics for top-quark backgrounds, nominal distributions are compared against alternative MC configurations. This strategy probes the uncertainties linked to the hard-scattering matrix element calculation (comparing \textsc{Powheg} against \textsc{MadGraph5\_aMC@NLO}) as well as the underlying parton shower algorithm (contrasting \textsc{Powheg}+\textsc{Pythia}~8 with \textsc{Powheg}+\textsc{Herwig}~7). These generator-level variations typically induce an uncertainty of around 1\%.

\item Systematic deviations stemming from the choice of parton distribution functions (PDFs) are evaluated across all simulated signal and background datasets. The resulting variations on the expected yields are found to be contained within 1\% to 2\%.

\end{itemize}

On the experimental side, systematic sources encompass potential mis-modelings of lepton identification, reconstruction, and trigger efficiencies, alongside uncertainties in their corresponding momentum or energy scales and resolutions. Furthermore, variations associated with jet calibrations, the $E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ soft-term computation, $b$-tagging performance, pile-up modeling, and the integrated luminosity measurement are comprehensively incorporated. The directional impact of each individual experimental uncertainty upon the signal yield is quantified via explicit $+1\sigma$ (``up'') and $-1\sigma$ (``down'') shifts. Cumulatively, the overall experimental systematic uncertainty assigned to any given signal sample does not exceed 2.5\%.

Extensive evaluations of the global experimental uncertainties affecting the predicted yields of both the simplified DM frameworks and the 2HDM+$a$ models have been conducted. Throughout the expansive grid of simulated mass hypotheses, the magnitude of these detector-level effects exhibits remarkable stability. Specifically, the aggregated systematic variations for the simplified models generally fluctuate between 2\% and 3\%, occasionally reaching 5\% at certain extreme phase-space boundaries. A congruent pattern is observed for the 2HDM+$a$ signals, where cumulative experimental uncertainties typically reside within the 2\% to 4\% interval, topping out near 6\%. Given this relative uniformity across hundreds of discrete signal points, a summarized description is provided in text rather than an exhaustive tabulation. Ultimately, during the statistical limit extraction, the full shape-dependent (binned) variations for every considered systematic source are meticulously applied.

Regarding specific sub-dominant background contributions within the designated CRs, their associated systematic errors are extrapolated directly from SR evaluations. Consequently, the assigned normalization uncertainty for $\text{Z}$+jets events is inflated to 38\% across both the $e\mu$ and $3\ell$ boundaries, whereas the $\text{WZ}$ prediction within the $e\mu$ CR is subjected to a 9\% uncertainty. Furthermore, the systematic margins for the $\text{ZZ} \to \ell\ell\nu\nu$ process within the $e\mu$ and $3\ell$ regions are fixed at 6.6\%, compared to an 8.8\% assignment for the $\text{ZZ} \to 4\ell$ yield. Non-resonant background estimations populating the $3\ell$ CR are allocated a 14\% uncertainty. Lastly, highly conservative uncertainty bands of 50\% and 20\% are applied to the minimal triboson and $t\bar{t}\text{V}$ components, respectively.
