\section{Particle Physics}

This thesis presents studies in particle physics conducted with the ATLAS experiment at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC)~\cite{Bruning:2004}. Particle physics (high-energy physics) aims to understand the fundamental building blocks of the universe and the forces governing their interactions. It focuses on elementary particles, such as quarks and electrons, and investigates their behavior through high-energy particle collisions produced at accelerators like the LHC. By analyzing the products of these collisions, researchers test the predictions of the Standard Model (SM) of particle physics, summarized in a book~\cite{Quigg2013}, and search for new phenomena that may shed light on open questions, including the nature of dark matter and the underlying structure of space and time.

The study of particle physics has a long history spanning nearly two centuries. Over this time, physicists have established that the universe is governed by four fundamental forces, which act universally and influence phenomena ranging from everyday experiences, such as walking, telecommunications, and radiation therapy, to advanced technologies like nuclear energy production. These four fundamental forces---gravity, electromagnetism, the weak nuclear force, and the strong nuclear force---are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:four-force}.

In the 1960s, the electromagnetic and weak forces were unified within the SM theoretical framework, representing a breakthrough in our understanding of particle physics. 
The weak interaction was first postulated in the 1930s to explain beta decay, though its mediator particles remained unknown. In the 1950s, the Wu experiment~\cite{Wu1957} demonstrated parity (P) violation, revealing that the weak interaction possesses a fundamental ``handedness'' and does not conserve parity spatial symmetry. Building on the framework of Yang--Mills theory~\cite{YangMills1954}, Sheldon Glashow~\cite{Glashow:1961tr}, Steven Weinberg~\cite{Weinberg:1967tq}, and Abdus Salam~\cite{Salam:1968rm} formulated the electroweak theory between 1964 and 1971, unifying the electromagnetic and weak forces. The theory predicted the $\text{W}^\pm$ bosons, mediators of charged-current weak interactions, and the $\text{Z}$ boson, mediator of neutral-current weak interactions. The masses of these particles remained unknown for over a decade until their discovery in 1983 at the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) in the UA1 and UA2 experiments~\cite{UA1_1983_W, UA2_1983_W}, providing the first major confirmation of the SM. To explain how these gauge bosons acquire mass while leaving the photon massless, the theory incorporated the Brout--Englert--Higgs mechanism~\cite{EnglertBrout1964, Higgs1964}. This formulation predicted the existence of a massive scalar particle, the Higgs boson, whose momentous discovery nearly half a century later in 2012 by the ATLAS and CMS experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC)~\cite{ATLAS2012Higgs, CMS2012Higgs} completed the Standard Model picture of electroweak interactions.

The component of the SM that describes the strong force, Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), was developed in the early 1970s by Harald Fritzsch, Murray Gell-Mann, and Heinrich Leutwyler~\cite{Fritzsch1973QCD}. QCD proposes that quarks carry ``color'' charges (red, green, and blue) and are bound together by gluons. The discovery of asymptotic freedom in 1973~\cite{GrossWilczek1973,Politzer1973} explained why the strong force weakens at high energies, providing a fundamental understanding of quark interactions.

The SM represents our current understanding of particle physics and stands as one of the great triumphs of modern science. Figure~\ref{fig:sm} shows all the elementary particles, along with their key properties, including electric charge, spin, and mass.
%
Elementary particles are classified as either fermions or bosons in the SM. Fermions (quarks and leptons in three generations) are spin-$\frac{1}{2}$ particles that follow Fermi--Dirac statistics, constituting most of the visible physical matter in the universe. 
The gauge bosons are spin-1 particles that serve as the force carriers in the SM:
Gluons ($g$) mediate the strong interaction. They are also massless and electrically neutral.
Photons ($\gamma$) mediate electromagnetic interactions. They are massless and electrically neutral.
$\text{W}^\pm$ and $\text{Z}$ bosons mediate the weak interactions. These are massive particles.
Additionally, the Higgs boson is a spin-0 scalar particle responsible for generating mass for other particles (except neutrinos) through the Higgs mechanism~\cite{Higgs1964,EnglertBrout1964,GuralnikHagenKibble1964}.

Gravity is not included in the Standard Model (SM) primarily because a renormalizable quantum theory of gravity remains elusive. Practically, however, its exclusion is justified because its effects are extremely weak and negligible at subatomic scales. Because gravitational interactions scale with mass rather than gauge charge, their relative weakness compared to electromagnetism varies significantly depending on the interacting particles: approximately $10^{36}$ times weaker between two protons, $10^{39}$ times weaker between an electron and a proton, and $10^{43}$ times weaker between two electrons. These effects are essentially impossible to measure in current particle physics experiments. As a result, the SM remains highly accurate for describing particle interactions without incorporating gravity.
%%%%%%%%%%%

Today, experiments at the LHC, such as ATLAS~\cite{ATLAS2008}, continue to test the SM with unprecedented precision.
One of the primary physics goals of LHC experiments is the measurement of diboson production, such as $\text{ZZ}$, to probe the electroweak symmetry breaking mechanism, which is closely related to the fundamental question of how particles acquire mass. This topic forms a major part of the research presented in this PhD thesis.

Precise measurements of diboson production provide stringent tests of the SM and place constraints on physics beyond the SM (BSM), one of the most compelling challenges in modern particle physics. Because the SM does not account for Dark Matter (DM), which constitutes roughly 27\% of the universe's mass-energy content according to astrophysical observations~\cite{Bertone2005DMReview,Planck2018CosmoParams}, it motivates BSM theories proposing new particles and mechanisms to explain the nature of DM. Searches for dark matter at the LHC, using indirect signatures such as missing transverse energy and deviations from known SM processes, form another major component of the research presented in this thesis.

Many BSM models introduce particles that could serve as dark matter candidates. These particles are expected to be neutral and weakly interacting, so they typically leave no direct signals in detectors. Consequently, dark matter is detected indirectly, either through the decay products of visible particles or via the invisible component inferred from known processes. At the LHC, these searches rely heavily on precise measurements of missing transverse energy ($E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$) and detailed constraints from established SM process decay ratios.
%
Achieving this requires high-precision measurements of SM processes, as any deviations from expected behavior may indicate the presence of dark matter particles produced in the colliding beam data. Such precision is essential for identifying subtle dark matter signals within the complex background of SM events.

\section{Experiments at the Energy Frontier}

The LHC is the world’s largest high-energy particle collider, designed to explore particle physics at the energy frontier. Experiments at the energy frontier are essential for directly producing and studying new, massive subatomic particles, probing the fundamental structure of matter at the smallest scales, and recreating the extreme conditions of the early universe. These high-energy collisions enable searches for dark matter, extra spatial dimensions~\cite{ArkaniHamed1998ADD1,ArkaniHamed1998ADD2,RandallSundrum1999RS1,RandallSundrum1999RS2}, and insights into why matter dominates over antimatter.
%
Designing and building experiments at the energy frontier allows physicists to address fundamental questions in particle physics. High collision energies, as described by Einstein’s relation $E=mc^2$, enable the direct production of massive, previously unknown particles and the discovery of new forces or particles, such as those predicted by supersymmetry~\cite{WessZumino1974,GolfandLikhtman1971,SalamStrathdee1974} for additional heavy bosons. Through wave--particle duality, higher particle energies correspond to shorter de Broglie wavelengths~\cite{deBroglie1924Letter}, $\lambda=h/p$, allowing high-energy particles to probe smaller distance scales within matter. In addition, high-energy collisions recreate the extreme conditions that existed shortly after the Big Bang, providing insight into the fundamental interactions that shaped the early universe. Experiments at the energy frontier also enable precise studies of the SM and searches for phenomena beyond the SM.

Designing and building the accelerator and its detectors is a massive undertaking, involving very large and complex mechanical, electrical, materials research, and engineering efforts, and requiring more than a decade for the LHC and its experiments. An introduction to the LHC and the ATLAS detector is presented in this thesis Chapter~\ref{chap:experiment}.
The author of this thesis had the opportunity to participate in a large muon detector construction project for the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC)~\cite{Apollinari:2015bam}, which is expected to begin operations around 2030. Through this multi-year design and construction effort, the author gained extensive experience in detector hardware design, construction, control systems, and testing procedures. Detailed descriptions of this work are presented in Chapter~\ref{chap:sMDT}.

\section{Physics Analysis Approach at the LHC Experiment}

Physics data analysis at LHC experiments relies heavily on integrated luminosity, which quantifies the amount of data collected and enables precision measurements. The analysis itself is a structured, multi-step process designed to extract rare and interesting signal events from the vast background of ordinary collisions, transforming raw detector data into meaningful scientific results. This process begins with Monte Carlo (MC) simulations that model both the underlying physics processes and the detector response, followed by data-taking with appropriate triggers, reconstruction of physics objects, event selection, and statistical analysis to test the SM or search for new physics.

Major steps in LHC physics analysis are briefly described below.
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Event Simulation}: Before analyzing real data, physicists use simulations to model how specific physical processes (e.g., $pp \to \text{ZZ} \to 2\ell 2\nu$) should look in the detector.
\item \textbf{Trigger System and Data Acquisition}: The LHC produces millions of collisions per second, too many to record. A multi-level trigger system (real-time filtering) selects only the most interesting events.
\item \textbf{Object Reconstruction and Calibration}: Raw data (detector hits, energy deposits) are converted into physical objects: electrons, muons, photons, jets of hadrons, and missing transverse energy. Detectors are calibrated using known particle decays to ensure high precision. Details are described in Chapter~\ref{chap:recon}.
\item \textbf{Event Selection and Background Suppression}: Analysts apply cuts on kinematic variables (e.g., transverse momentum, angles) to enhance the signal-to-background ratio. This is crucial for isolating the desired physics, such as the cross-section measurement of the $\text{ZZ} \to 2\ell 2\nu$ channel.
\item \textbf{Background Estimation}: Estimating the background (often from standard model processes) is critical, usually done using a combination of simulation and data-driven methods from control regions.
\item \textbf{Systematic Uncertainties}: Evaluation of how detector imperfections, calibration errors, and theoretical uncertainties affect the final result. 
\item \textbf{Statistical Analysis and Interpretation}: A rigorous mathematical framework is employed to interpret the selected data events:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Likelihood Functions}: A probability model~\cite{Fisher1922} is constructed (often using histograms/templates) to model the data as a sum of expected signal and background components.
    \item \textit{Statistical Inference}: The profile maximum likelihood method is widely used to measure parameters of interest (e.g., production cross-sections) or to set upper limits on new physics parameters.
    \item \textit{Significance}: Results are quantified in terms of standard deviations ($\sigma$) to determine if an observed excess constitutes a discovery ($>5\sigma$) or merely a statistical fluctuation of the background.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}


The experimental principles and analytical steps outlined above establish the foundation for the three primary research pillars presented in this thesis. Rather than acting as isolated studies, these three projects form a cohesive, multi-pronged strategy aimed at exploring the frontiers of particle physics at the LHC:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Precision Measurements as an Indirect Probe (Chapter~\ref{chap:zz})}: The precision measurement of $\text{ZZ}$ production in the $\ell\ell\nu\nu$ final state tests the boundaries of the Standard Model (SM) to an unprecedented degree. By scrutinizing these electroweak processes, we look for subtle deviations from SM predictions, which serve as a powerful indirect indicator of potential new physics.
    \item \textbf{Direct Searches within Specific New Physics Models (Chapter~\ref{chap:monoZ})}: Complementing the precision framework, the search for Dark Matter (DM) in the $\text{ZH}$ production channel ($\ell\ell + E_{\text{T}}^{\text{miss}}$ final state) targets a well-motivated, specific Beyond-the-Standard-Model (BSM) scenario. This allows for a direct, model-dependent exploration of dark sector interactions.
    \item {\bf Advanced Instrumentation for Future Discovery Sensitivity (Chapter~\ref{chap:sMDT})}: To sustain and maximize the experimental sensitivity for both precision measurements and new physics searches under future high-luminosity conditions, a substantial detector upgrade is imperative. The final part of this thesis focuses on the detector R\&D and upgrades necessary to maintain high reconstruction efficiency and robust background rejection within the challenging high-pileup environment of the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC).
\end{itemize}

In summary, this thesis spans from testing the limits of the SM via high-precision data to directly searching for specific new physics signatures, all while developing the next-generation detector infrastructure essential for securing these scientific goals. More importantly, compared to previous studies by the ATLAS and CMS collaborations, as well as recent underground dark matter experiments, the work presented here achieves significant advancements:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unprecedented Precision in SM Tests:} By utilizing the full LHC Run 2 dataset ($140~\text{fb}^{-1}$), the inclusive $\text{ZZ}$ fiducial cross-section measurement achieves a precision of 4.6\%, a substantial improvement over the 7.0\% precision of previous measurements based on partial datasets. Furthermore, constraints on the SMEFT coefficients for anomalous neutral triple gauge couplings (aTGCs) are derived for the first time in the $\ell\ell\nu\nu$ channel, marking a state-of-the-art test of the electroweak sector.
    \item \textbf{Highly Competitive Dark Matter Constraints:} In the Mono-$\text{Z}$ channel, the search sensitivity is significantly enhanced. The upper limit on the Higgs boson invisible decay branching ratio $\mathcal{B}(\text{H} \to \text{inv.})$ is tightened to 18.7\% at 95\% CL (compared to 67\% in previous $36.1~\text{fb}^{-1}$ results). The derived exclusion limits for simplified DM models and 2HDM+$a$ models not only surpass previous collider results but also show strong complementarity to underground direct-detection experiments, exhibiting superior sensitivity particularly in the low DM mass regime.
    \item \textbf{Readiness for the HL-LHC Era:} The successful construction, stringent quality control, and excellent cosmic-ray test performance (single-tube spatial resolution $< 90~\mu\text{m}$) of 50 sMDT chambers ensure that the ATLAS muon spectrometer can maintain optimal tracking resolution and trigger efficiency under the extreme background rates of the upcoming High-Luminosity LHC.
\end{itemize}

Through these three interconnected pillars—pushing the precision frontier, expanding the discovery reach for dark matter, and upgrading the fundamental detector hardware—this thesis provides a comprehensive and advanced contribution to the ongoing exploration of particle physics at the energy frontier.
